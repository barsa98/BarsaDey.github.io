Beginnings

It began on a sunny morning during one of my days in 8th standard, when we first observed hibiscus leaves under the compound microscope in our biology lab. What started as simple curiosity quickly turned into fascination as we visualized the intricate vein patterns inside the leaves along with the tiny stomata scattered across their surface. That excitement only grew as we continued exploring more specimens-flowers, ovaries, pollen grains, pond water filled with green algae, and almost anything we could collect from our surroundings. Each observation felt like discovering a hidden world that had always existed around us but had remained unseen to the naked eye. That early curiosity eventually guided me toward pursuing both my bachelor’s and master’s degrees in Molecular Biology and Biotechnology.

Wanderings

My research journey began in 2019 when I got the opportunity to intern at the Defence Research Laboratory (DRDO), Tezpur. During this time, I explored experimental biomedical research by studying plant-derived compounds and their potential therapeutic effects using Wistar rat models. This experience introduced me to phytochemical extraction, biochemical assays, and the process of observing biological responses in vivo.

In 2021, I got the opportunity to join the All India Institute of Medical Sciences (AIIMS), New Delhi as a summer research intern. Here, I explored a more data-driven side of biomedical research by mining scientific literature and biochemical databases to identify gaps in disease pathways. This experience helped me understand how large repositories of biological data can guide meaningful research questions.

During my master’s dissertation at Tezpur University (2022), I explored computational approaches to studying neurological disorders. My work focused on investigating the aggregation mechanisms of amyloid-beta (Aβ42), a key factor in neurodegenerative diseases, using molecular docking and molecular dynamics simulations. This project allowed me to connect molecular-level computational modeling with biological questions related to disease pathology.

Later, I got the opportunity to join the Indian Institute of Science Education and Research (IISER), Berhampur as a Junior Research Fellow (2023–2024), where I explored the analysis of clinical neuroimaging data. During this time, I worked on MRI preprocessing, quality control across imaging modalities, and explored the use of machine learning models to study patterns in neuroimaging datasets.

Building on this experience, I continued at IISER Berhampur as a Project Research Scientist–I (2025). In this role, I explored deep learning and mathematical approaches for multimodal MRI image translation. I developed reproducible workflows for medical image analysis and evaluated model performance using quantitative metrics such as SSIM, PSNR, MSE, and SMAPE, with the aim of improving the reliability of computational pipelines used in clinical neuroimaging research.
